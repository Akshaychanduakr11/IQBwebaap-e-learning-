UNIT 1

UNIT 1: Battery Technology & Energy Conversion


A. Fundamental Electrochemistry

  • Electrochemical Series: A list of elements arranged in increasing order of their standard reduction potentials ($E^0$).
  • Significance: Elements at the top (like Lithium) are strong reducing agents, while those at the bottom (like Fluorine) are strong oxidizing agents.
  • Galvanic Series: Based on practical testing in specific environments like seawater; helps engineers predict corrosion.
  • Galvanic (Voltaic) Cell: Converts chemical energy into electrical energy (Spontaneous reaction).
  • Electrolytic Cell: Converts electrical energy into chemical energy (Non-spontaneous reaction).

B. Battery Technology

A battery consists of one or more electrochemical cells, classified as:

  • Primary Batteries: Non-rechargeable; irreversible reactions (e.g., Zinc-Carbon).
  • Secondary Batteries: Rechargeable; reversible reactions (e.g., Lithium-ion).
  • Reserve Batteries: Electrolyte is kept separate until needed for a long shelf life (missiles, life vests).

Lithium-Cobalt Oxide ($LiCoO_2$) Battery:

  • Components: Anode (Graphite), Cathode (Lithium Cobalt Oxide), and Electrolyte (Lithium salt).
  • Working: $Li^+$ ions move from Anode to Cathode during discharge and back during charging (intercalation).
  • Applications: Portable electronics and laptops.

Metal-Air Batteries (e.g., Zinc-Air):

  • Concept: Uses atmospheric oxygen as the cathode, making it very light.
  • Characteristics: Extremely high energy density.

C. Supercapacitors

  • EDLC: Stores energy electrostatically via charge separation; no chemical reaction.
  • Pseudo-capacitors: Stores energy through fast redox reactions at the surface.
  • Asymmetric Capacitors: Combines EDLC and battery-like electrodes for high power and energy.

D. Energy Conversion Devices

  • $H_2-O_2$ Fuel Cells: Converts chemical energy of Hydrogen fuel directly into electricity. Byproduct is only Water.
  • Amorphous Silicon (a-Si) Solar Cells: Non-crystalline silicon; flexible and cost-effective.
  • Quantum Dye-Sensitized Solar Cells (QDSSCs): Uses quantum dots to absorb sunlight; works in low-light conditions.

UNIT 2

UNIT 2: Rare Earth Elements & E-Waste Management


A. Rare Earth Elements (REEs) & Lanthanides

Rare Earth Elements consist of 17 chemical elements: the 15 lanthanides plus scandium and yttrium. In engineering, they are indispensable due to their unique 4f electron configurations.

  • Electronic Properties: Their ability to handle high temperatures and provide strong magnetic fields makes them vital for NdFeB (Neodymium) magnets and superconductors.
  • Lanthanide Contraction: This is the cumulative decrease in atomic and ionic radii from Lanthanum ($La^{3+}$) to Lutetium ($Lu^{3+}$).
    — Cause: The 4f electrons provide poor shielding of the nuclear charge, allowing the nucleus to pull the outer electrons closer.
    — Impact: It results in the high density of post-lanthanide elements (like Tungsten and Gold) and makes the separation of REEs a complex chemical challenge.

B. E-Waste Management & Metal Extraction

E-waste (Electronic Waste) is the fastest-growing waste stream. It is a "Double-Edged Sword": it contains environmental toxins (Lead, Cadmium) but also Precious Metals (Gold, Platinum).

  • Environmental Risks: Leaching of Brominated Flame Retardants (BFRs) and heavy metals into groundwater causing bioaccumulation.
  • The Recycling Hierarchy:
    • Pyrometallurgical: High-temperature smelting. It is fast but releases toxic gases and has high energy costs.
    • Hydrometallurgical: The use of acidic or alkaline leaching (using $HNO_3$ or $HCl$) to dissolve metals into a solution, followed by solvent extraction.
    • Direct Recycling: Physical separation of components to maintain their chemical integrity.

C. Green Chemistry & Green Computing

This section focuses on the Sustainable Engineering approach to design.

The 12 Principles of Green Chemistry (Key Highlights):

  1. Waste Prevention: It is better to prevent waste than to treat it.
  2. Atom Economy: Synthetic methods should maximize the incorporation of all materials used into the final product.
  3. Design for Energy Efficiency: Minimize energy requirements (conduct reactions at ambient pressure/temp).
  4. Renewable Feedstocks: Use raw materials that are renewable rather than depleting.

Green Computing:

  • Role: Reducing the power consumption of Data Servers (which account for 2% of global electricity).
  • Green Devices: Using Energy Star rated hardware and "sleep mode" algorithms.
  • Research: Developing Green Algorithms that reduce the CPU cycles needed for complex engineering simulations.

UNIT 3

UNIT 3: Nanomaterials & Carbon Nanotechnology


A. Introduction to Nanomaterials

Nanomaterials are substances where at least one dimension is in the range of 1 nm to 100 nm ($1 \text{ nm} = 10^{-9} \text{ m}$). At this scale, materials stop behaving like bulk matter and start obeying Quantum Mechanics.

Classification based on Dimensions:

  • 0-D (Zero Dimensional): All dimensions are at the nanoscale (e.g., Quantum Dots, Nanoparticles).
  • 1-D (One Dimensional): Two dimensions are at the nanoscale (e.g., Nanowires, Nanotubes).
  • 2-D (Two Dimensional): One dimension is at the nanoscale (e.g., Graphene, Nanosheets).
  • 3-D (Three Dimensional): Bulk materials composed of nanostructures.

B. Size Dependent Properties

As materials shrink to the nanoscale, two major factors change their behavior:

  • Surface Area Effect: The Surface-to-Volume Ratio increases dramatically. This makes nanomaterials exceptionally reactive, making them excellent Catalysts.
  • Optical Properties: Gold ($Au$) at the nanoscale isn't "golden"; it appears red or purple due to Surface Plasmon Resonance (SPR).
  • Electronic Properties: The bandgap of semiconductors changes with size, allowing us to "tune" the color of LEDs.

C. Synthesis Methods: Top-Down & Bottom-Up

Engineering these materials requires two distinct philosophical approaches:

Approach Method Examples
Top-Down Breaking bulk material into nano-size. Ball Milling, Lithography, Etching.
Bottom-Up Assembling atom-by-atom or molecule-by-molecule. Sol-Gel, CVD, Self-assembly.

D. Carbon Nanomaterials: Graphene & CNTs

  • Graphene: A single, 2D layer of carbon atoms arranged in a honeycomb lattice ($sp^2$ hybridized). It is 200 times stronger than steel and an excellent conductor.
  • Carbon Nanotubes (CNTs): These are essentially rolled-up sheets of graphene.
    • SWCNT: Single-Walled (Single tube).
    • MWCNT: Multi-Walled (Nested tubes like a "Russian Doll").
  • Properties: High Young’s Modulus ($~1 \text{ TPa}$), high thermal conductivity, and can be metallic or semiconducting depending on their Chirality.

UNIT 4

UNIT 4: Material Characterization Techniques


A. Spectroscopy (Electronic, IR, and NMR)

Spectroscopy is the study of the interaction between Electromagnetic Radiation and matter.

  • Electronic Spectroscopy (UV-Vis): Based on the Beer-Lambert Law ($A = \epsilon bc$), which relates the absorption of light to the properties of the material through which the light is traveling.
    Application: Determining the concentration of a substance in a solution.
  • Infra-Red (IR) Spectroscopy: Measures the vibrations of molecular bonds. Each functional group (like -OH or -C=O) absorbs a specific frequency of IR light.
    Application: Identifying functional groups in organic compounds (The "Fingerprint" region).
  • Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR): Uses strong magnetic fields to observe the local magnetic fields around atomic nuclei (usually Hydrogen-1 or Carbon-13).
    Application: Determining the 3D structure of complex molecules.

B. Thermal Analysis Techniques

How do materials behave when they are heated or cooled?

  • Thermogravimetry (TGA): Measures change in weight as a function of temperature.
    Use: Studying thermal stability and decomposition temperature.
  • Differential Scanning Calorimetry (DSC): Measures the heat flow difference between a sample and a reference.
    Use: Finding the Glass Transition Temperature ($T_g$) and Melting Point ($T_m$).

C. Advanced Microscopy (SEM, TEM, AFM)

When light microscopes reach their limit, we use electrons or physical probes to "see" atoms.

  • Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM): Scans the surface with an electron beam to create 3D-like images of the topography.
  • Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM): Passes electrons through a very thin sample. It provides atomic-level resolution of internal structures.
  • Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM): Uses a mechanical cantilever (probe) to "feel" the surface at the atomic scale.

D. Surface Analysis & Chromatography

  • BET Surface Area Analysis: Uses gas adsorption (usually Nitrogen) to calculate the specific surface area of porous materials (essential for Unit 3 Nanomaterials).
  • X-Ray Diffraction (XRD): Uses X-rays to determine the crystal structure and phase purity of a solid material.
  • Chromatography (HPLC & Gas): Techniques used to separate complex mixtures into individual components based on their speed through a stationary phase.

UNIT 5

UNIT 5: Polymers & Advanced Engineering Materials


A. Introduction to Polymers

Polymers are high-molecular-weight macromolecules built from repeating units called Monomers. The process of linking these monomers is called Polymerization.

  • Thermoplastics: Polymers that soften on heating and can be remolded (e.g., PVC, Polythene). They have linear or branched structures.
  • Thermosetting Plastics: Polymers that undergo permanent chemical change on heating and cannot be remolded (e.g., Bakelite, Epoxy resins). They have a 3D cross-linked structure.
  • Glass Transition Temperature ($T_g$): The temperature at which an amorphous polymer transitions from a hard, glassy state to a soft, rubbery state.
[Image of thermoplastic vs thermosetting plastic molecular structure]

B. Specialty & Conducting Polymers

Modern engineering requires "Smart Materials" that do more than just provide structure.

  • Conducting Polymers: Organic polymers that conduct electricity (e.g., Polyaniline, Polypyrrole). They are used in biosensors and flexible electronics.
  • Biodegradable Polymers: Materials like PLA (Polylactic Acid) that break down naturally, reducing environmental impact.
  • Liquid Crystal Polymers (LCPs): Materials that exist in a state between solid and liquid, used in high-resolution LCD displays.

C. Composites & Alloys

A composite is a material made from two or more constituent materials with significantly different physical or chemical properties.

  • Matrix Phase: The continuous phase (e.g., Polymer, Metal, or Ceramic).
  • Reinforcement Phase: Added to improve strength (e.g., Glass fibers, Carbon fibers).
  • FRP (Fiber Reinforced Plastics): High strength-to-weight ratio materials used in aircraft wings and wind turbine blades.

D. Industrial Materials: Lubricants & Refractories

  • Lubricants: Substances used to reduce friction between moving surfaces.
    Mechanism: Fluid film, Boundary, and Extreme Pressure lubrication.
  • Refractories: Materials that can withstand very high temperatures without melting or decomposing.
    Application: Lining of furnaces and kilns in steel manufacturing.
