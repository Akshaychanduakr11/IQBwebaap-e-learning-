Modern AI Paradigms

Knowledge Engineering & Generative Systems

01. Knowledge Representation (KR)

For an AI to reason, it needs a way to represent the world. Unlike raw data, KR focuses on Semantics (meaning) and Logic.

// First-Order Logic (FOL) Example
∀x Man(x) ⇒ Mortal(x) // Axiom: All men are mortal
Man(Socrates) // Fact: Socrates is a man
∴ Mortal(Socrates) // Inference: Socrates is mortal

Semantic Networks: Graphical representations showing relationships between objects.
Frames: Data structures for representing stereotypical situations (e.g., a "Hotel" frame).

02. Expert Systems

Systems that mimic human expertise using IF-THEN rules. They consist of a Knowledge Base and an Inference Engine.

  • Forward Chaining: Data-driven; starts with facts to reach a conclusion.
  • Backward Chaining: Goal-driven; starts with a hypothesis and looks for supporting facts.

03. Generative AI & LLMs

Modern AI uses the Transformer architecture to create new content. LLMs process data through tokenization and latent space vectors.

// Self-Attention Concept
"The bank was closed because the river overflowed."
// The model connects "bank" to "river" to understand context.

04. AI Ethics & Safety

Focuses on the Alignment Problem—ensuring AI systems act in accordance with human values and safety standards.

  • Algorithmic Bias: Prejudices inherited from training data.
  • Explainable AI (XAI): Making "Black Box" models understandable to humans.

Exam Prep: AI Deep Dive

A test of a machine's ability to exhibit intelligent behavior indistinguishable from a human. If a human judge cannot tell the difference between the machine and a person, the machine passes.
